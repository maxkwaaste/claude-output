I. Before "Race": Human Difference in the Ancient World

The concept of grouping humans by physical appearance and ranking them hierarchically is not ancient. Older civilizations organized difference along entirely different lines: language, religion, customs, geography, and political membership. The shift from cultural to biological classification of humans happened slowly, over roughly 500 years (1400-1900), and the moral condemnation of that classification happened in about 30 years (1935-1965).

Greeks and Romans: Barbarians, not races

The word "barbarian" (barbaros) originally meant "someone who doesn't speak Greek." Their speech sounded like "bar-bar" to Greek ears. The distinction was linguistic and cultural, not biological. Aristotle's "natural slaves" (Politics, Book I) were a civic and climatic category: he believed peoples in cold climates were brave but stupid, those in warm climates intelligent but servile, and Greeks occupied the sweet spot. A barbarian could, in theory, become civilized by adopting Greek customs. This is the opposite of racial thinking, where biology is destiny.

Ancient Egypt and Persia

Ancient Egyptians identified people by culture, language, geography, and status, not skin color. Rebecca Futo Kennedy (Barnard College) argues that asking whether Egyptians were "black" or "white" is anachronistic: it "says more about modern political investments than attempting to understand antiquity on its own terms." The Achaemenid Persian Empire (550-330 BCE) celebrated the diversity of its subject peoples as a demonstration of imperial power. Royal inscriptions at Persepolis listed dozens of peoples in distinctive dress. The Persian approach was administrative: peoples were categorized by region, language, and tribute status.

Ancient China: The Hua-Yi distinction

Chinese civilization developed the Hua-Yi distinction (civilized Chinese vs. "barbarians" in the four cardinal directions), crystallizing during the Eastern Zhou period (770-256 BCE). Critically, this was cultural, not racial. Tang-dynasty Confucian Han Yu wrote: "If the feudal lords use the Yi ritual, then they should be called Yi. If they use Chinese rituals, then they should be called Chinese." Barbarians became Chinese through assimilation. Historian Frank Dikotter dates the Chinese "idea of race" (as opposed to culture) to the late 19th-century Qing dynasty, with the transition completing only in the 1920s.

The Curse of Ham: A slow racialization

Genesis 9:20-27 contains no mention of skin color or race. Noah curses Canaan to be "a servant of servants." The racialization happened in stages:

  • Babylonian Talmud (~500 AD): Discusses Ham's sin but does not describe skin punishment as hereditary or linked to slavery.
  • Cave of Treasures (6th-7th century): First suggestion that Canaan's descendants were dark-skinned.
  • Arab Islamic scholars (9th-10th century): Al-Tabari and al-Yaqubi developed the "dual curse of blackness and slavery." In 9th-century Arabia, where most enslaved people were Black Ethiopians, this allowed race to become systematically associated with slavery for the first time.
  • European colonialism (16th-17th century): The explicitly racist interpretation, that God willed Black people to be enslaved permanently, first surfaces in European writing.
  • American pro-slavery (18th-19th century): Deployed widely. Abandoned by even conservative theologians by the mid-20th century.
Key Finding It took over 2,000 years for a Bible story about a family argument to become a racial justification for slavery. The racialization of the Curse of Ham was not inherent in the text. Each era's interpreters projected their own social arrangements onto it. The Arab slave trade produced the first racialized reading; European colonialism amplified it; American slavery weaponized it.

II. The First Racial Law: Limpieza de Sangre (1449)

On June 5, 1449, Pero de Sarmiento issued the Sentencia-Estatuto in Toledo, barring conversos (Jewish converts to Christianity) from holding public or private office, regardless of the sincerity of their faith. The criterion was not belief but ancestry: four generations of Christian affiliation were required. Anti-Semitism expert Leon Poliakov called this "the first example in history of legalized racism."

The logic was straightforward and chilling: conversos had converted. They attended Mass. They were baptized. Religion was no longer the distinguishing marker. What remained was blood. And blood was the criterion they chose.

Others urge caution, noting that 17th-century Europe was not yet thinking in modern racial categories. But the structural logic is hard to deny: when you discriminate against people who have already changed their religion, the only remaining basis is ancestry. That is the logic of race.

III. The Invention of "Race" as a Scientific Concept

The word itself

The etymology of "race" is uncertain. Spanish raza and Italian razza appeared in the 15th century; French race in 1512; English race in the 16th century. Six different etymologies have been proposed, none proven: Latin radix (root), Latin ratio (breed value), Arabic ras (head), French haras (stud farm). Shakespeare used "race" for royal lineage. The word meant "family" or "nation" for centuries before it acquired biological meaning.

The semantic shift from "lineage" to "biological type" was gradual: physical/phenotypical connotations in the 17th century, systematic taxonomy in the 18th, full biological meaning in the 19th.

The classifiers

Francois Bernier (1684)
First racial classification
French physician who spent a decade traveling across Mughal India. Published the first post-Classical classification of humans into distinct races by physical appearance and geography, not religion or civilization. Four categories: Europeans/North Africans/Middle Easterners/Indians (grouped together), sub-Saharan Africans, East Asians, and Sami. Predated Linnaeus by 50 years.
Carl Linnaeus (1735)
Systema Naturae
Classified humanity into four varieties with behavioral traits baked in. Homo europaeus: "light, wise, inventors." Homo asiaticus: "stern, haughty, greedy." Homo afer: "sly, sluggish, neglectful." A monogenist (one species), but his taxonomy embedded value judgments that later racial scientists eagerly exploited.
Johann Friedrich Blumenbach (1775)
Five races, but misrepresented
Established the five-race classification: Caucasian, Mongolian, Malayan, Ethiopian, American. Named "Caucasian" after a skull he considered beautiful. But Blumenbach insisted all differences were induced by environment, that they were "so gradual it was not possible to separate these varieties clearly," and that "individual Africans differ as much from other individual Africans as Europeans differ from Europeans." He supported the basic equality of all races. His classification was hijacked by later racial scientists who stripped away every qualification.

The Enlightenment paradox

The Enlightenment proclaimed universal human rights while simultaneously producing the most elaborate racial hierarchies in history. If natural rights are universal, how do you explain enslaved Africans? The answer Enlightenment thinkers reached: biological inferiority. Racial classification became the escape hatch from universalism.

  • Voltaire: An outspoken polygenist. "Whites ... Negroes ... the yellow races ... are not descended from the same man." Had direct financial investments in colonial enterprises, including the slave trade.
  • David Hume (1753): "I am apt to suspect the Negroes to be naturally inferior to the Whites. There scarcely ever was a civilized nation of that complexion." Challenged anyone to cite a counterexample.
  • Immanuel Kant (1775): The Negro "undoubtedly holds the lowest of all remaining levels." Dismissed a Black carpenter's views because "being completely black from head to foot" was "a distinct proof that what he said was stupid." The tension between his universalist moral philosophy and his explicit racial hierarchy remains unresolved.

19th century: Peak racial science

Arthur de Gobineau's Essay on the Inequality of the Human Races (1853-55) was the bible of racial ideology. Houston Stewart Chamberlain's Foundations of the Nineteenth Century (1899) became a personal favorite of the young Adolf Hitler. Samuel Morton ranked races by skull size. Herbert Spencer coined "survival of the fittest." Francis Galton coined "eugenics" (1883). By the 1930s, over 30 US states had compulsory sterilization laws and more than 60,000 people had been forcibly sterilized.

Key Finding Was there moral opposition to racial hierarchy before the 20th century? Yes, but it was limited and rarely egalitarian. Las Casas (1550) defended indigenous rationality. Montesquieu (1748) condemned slavery on natural law grounds. Frederick Douglass (1854) directly attacked craniology and polygenism. But most abolitionists argued slavery was wrong without arguing that races were equal. The full dismantling of scientific racism as a paradigm did not happen until the mid-20th century.

Key Finding The word "racism" originated in French, spread to English and Spanish, and exploded simultaneously across all five languages in 1933. French had measurable usage as early as 1894, during the Dreyfus Affair. English followed in 1902 (Richard Henry Pratt). But the word remained niche in every language until the Nazis took power. Hitler's rise forced every European language to find a word for what was happening. The 1933 spike is synchronous across all five corpora. Italian shows a distinctive second spike in 1938, when Mussolini introduced his racial laws. The German data is paradoxical: "Rassismus" spikes in 1933 but then drops, because the Nazis preferred their own terminology ("Rassenkunde," "Rassentheorie") to a word that was already being used to criticize them.

Who coined it?

The popular claim that Leon Trotsky invented the word "racism" is false. The actual chronology:

  • 1892: French "raciste" first documented in print.
  • 1894: Gaston Mery and Edouard Drumont used "racisme" in the antisemitic newspaper La Libre Parole. The original French meaning was closer to "nationalist racial chauvinist" than today's meaning.
  • 1897: Charles Malato, French anarchist, used "raciste" and "racisme" in Philosophie de l'anarchie.
  • 1902: Richard Henry Pratt, US Army Colonel, used "racism" in English at the Lake Mohonk Conference: "Association of races and classes is necessary in order to destroy racism and classism." (OED first English citation.)
  • 1928: James Strachey Barnes used "Racism" in The Universal Aspects of Fascism, calling it "a materialistic illusion contrary to natural law."
  • 1930: Trotsky used "rasistov" (racists) in The History of the Russian Revolution, referring to Slavophile nationalists. He borrowed from French, 38 years after the first French usage.
  • 1933-34: Magnus Hirschfeld wrote Rassismus, the first book-length critical analysis of racism as an ideology.

The word was born in France, in the antisemitic ferment of the 1890s Dreyfus Affair. It entered English as a reform-minded critique (Pratt, 1902), then became an analytical and moral category in the 1930s (Barnes, Hirschfeld) as the Nazis gave it urgent new relevance.

V. Jewish Voices: Who Built the Moral Framework?

The moral category of "racism," as something to be opposed rather than practiced, was constructed by many hands. But Jewish intellectuals and organizations played a disproportionate and documentable role in that construction. The causal logic is straightforward: people who had experienced, and in many cases barely survived, the most extreme consequences of racial ideology had both the motivation and the intellectual framework to dismantle it.

The intellectual lineage

Franz Boas (1858-1942)
German-Jewish anthropologist, Columbia University
Founded American cultural anthropology. His 1912 immigration study showed that skull shapes of immigrant children changed within a single generation, destroying the craniometric basis of scientific racism. Trained Ruth Benedict, Margaret Mead, Zora Neale Hurston. Organized a public statement signed by over 8,000 scientists declaring that "there is only one science, to which race and religion are irrelevant." Died in 1942, reportedly mid-sentence, warning about the dangers of racism.
Magnus Hirschfeld (1868-1935)
German-Jewish physician, named the phenomenon
Wrote Rassismus in French exile after fleeing Nazi Germany (1933-34). By creating the diagnostic label "racism" and treating it as a subject of critical study, he made racial ideology visible as an ideology. Before Hirschfeld, racial thinking was the default mode of Western science. The act of naming it was itself a political and intellectual act.
Ashley Montagu (1905-1999)
Born Israel Ehrenberg, London East End
Studied under Boas at Columbia. Published Man's Most Dangerous Myth: The Fallacy of Race (1942), arguing race is largely a social construction. Served as rapporteur (lead author) of the 1950 UNESCO Statement on Race, the most consequential institutional declaration against scientific racism in the 20th century. Bridged academic research and international policy.
Hannah Arendt (1906-1975)
German-Jewish political theorist, fled 1933
The Origins of Totalitarianism (1951) traced racism from 19th-century imperialism through antisemitism to totalitarian state murder. She showed how racial thinking evolved from pseudo-science to state policy to industrialized killing. Not just moral condemnation, but the most comprehensive genealogy of racial ideology as a technology of power.
Theodor Adorno & the Frankfurt School
Funded by American Jewish Committee
The Authoritarian Personality (1950) developed the F-scale to measure fascism-susceptibility. Part of the five-volume Studies in Prejudice series, commissioned and funded by the AJC's Department of Scientific Research and co-edited by Max Horkheimer. The largest and most systematic social-scientific investigation of prejudice ever undertaken. It psychologized racism as a personality disorder.
Raphael Lemkin (1900-1959)
Polish-Jewish lawyer, lost 49 family members
Coined "genocide" (1943/1944). Published Axis Rule in Occupied Europe (1944). Campaigned for the UN Convention on the Prevention and Punishment of the Crime of Genocide (adopted December 9, 1948). Connected racial ideology to its terminal consequence. By creating a legal term for the ultimate product of racial thinking, he gave international law a tool to prosecute it.
Claude Levi-Strauss (1908-2009)
French-Jewish anthropologist
Race and History (1952), commissioned by UNESCO. Argued cultural differences result from historical and geographical circumstances, not biology, and that no culture can claim objective superiority. Served on the 1950 UNESCO Statement on Race drafting committee.
Horace Kallen (1882-1974)
American philosopher of German-Jewish descent
Coined "cultural pluralism" (1915): "Democracy involves, not the elimination of differences, but the perfection and conservation of differences." Conceived in large part to address concerns about American Jewish identity. Provided the positive counterpart to anti-racism: where Boas dismantled the science, Kallen offered a philosophical framework for how multi-ethnic society could work.

The UNESCO Statements on Race (1950, 1951)

The 1950 UNESCO Statement on Race, issued July 18, 1950, was the most consequential institutional declaration against scientific racism in the 20th century. It was drafted in 1949 by a committee of eight experts. Of the eight, Ashley Montagu (born Israel Ehrenberg) served as rapporteur (lead drafter) and Claude Levi-Strauss (French-Jewish) was a committee member.

The statement declared: race is a biological fact but racial classifications are cultural constructs; there is no scientific basis for racial hierarchy; differences in achievement between groups are explained by cultural and social factors, not biology. It was controversial: four scientists (Darlington, Fisher, Genna, Coon) opposed it, leading to a revised 1951 statement with more cautious language. By 1967, a further statement was adopted "by consensus, without opposition or vote."

VI. The Holocaust as Catalyst

Part I of this analysis showed that "racism" went from 0.042 ppm to 0.359 ppm in English between 1935 and 1945, an 8.5x increase. The cross-language data now shows this spike was synchronous across all five tracked languages. The cause is not in doubt.

What the camps revealed

The liberation of Auschwitz (January 27, 1945), Buchenwald (April 11, 1945), and Bergen-Belsen (April 15, 1945) produced photographic and film evidence that made the consequences of racial ideology undeniable. "It was only after the world saw the images of Auschwitz and Buchenwald that scientists, gradually and reluctantly, began to seek out the flaws of scientific racism."

Nuremberg: Racial ideology as criminal

The Nuremberg Trials (November 1945 - October 1946) framed racial ideology not merely as wrong but as criminal, as the ideological infrastructure of crimes against humanity. American prosecutors used the Nazis' own documents: "they wanted to convict Nazi war criminals with their own words." By October 1946, 79% of Germans polled had heard about the verdicts and considered the trial fair. 71% said they had "learned something new."

The institutional response (1948-1952)

  • 1948: UN Universal Declaration of Human Rights. Its first ten provisions are "clearly a reaction to Nazi policies and include a repudiation of racism."
  • 1948: UN Convention on the Prevention and Punishment of the Crime of Genocide (Lemkin's achievement).
  • 1950: UNESCO Statement on Race (Montagu's achievement).
  • 1951: Arendt's Origins of Totalitarianism.
  • 1952: Levi-Strauss, Race and History for UNESCO.
Key Finding The Nazis made the implicit explicit. Racial hierarchy had been the unexamined default of Western science for over a century. The Nazis carried its logic to its endpoint: industrialized murder based on racial classification. The world recoiled. Within three years of the camps being liberated (1945-1948), the international community produced the Universal Declaration of Human Rights, the Genocide Convention, and began the process that led to the UNESCO Statement on Race. The entire moral and institutional framework for opposing racism was built in a compressed burst between 1945 and 1952.

Rabbi Abraham Joshua Heschel (1907-1972), a Polish-born theologian who fled Europe in 1940 (most of his family perished in the Holocaust), marched arm-in-arm with Martin Luther King from Selma to Montgomery on March 21, 1965. His remark: "I felt like I was praying with my legs." He had argued at a 1963 conference that "you can't actually be religious and racist." Andrew Goodman and Michael Schwerner, two Jewish civil rights workers, were murdered alongside James Chaney (Black) by the KKK in Mississippi during Freedom Summer 1964. The Exodus narrative served as a shared reference point for both Jewish and Black Americans.

The fracture

The alliance did not survive the shift from Southern desegregation to Northern urban power. The 1968 Ocean Hill-Brownsville crisis, where a community-controlled school board in Brooklyn (majority Black and Puerto Rican) clashed with the United Federation of Teachers (approximately 85% Jewish), became the turning point. As Black Power emphasized self-determination, the role of white allies, including Jewish allies, was questioned. Jewish teachers, landlords, and small business owners were often the most visible white authority figures in Black neighborhoods, creating friction that had nothing to do with ideology.

A further nuance: the civil rights alliance was overwhelmingly an Ashkenazi phenomenon (Eastern European Jews). Sephardi Jews had a different history: some Western European Sephardi families participated in colonization and slave-holding in the Americas. Mizrahi Jews (Middle Eastern/North African) had their own experience of racialized discrimination within Israel, leading to the formation of the Israeli Black Panthers in 1971.

IX. The Full Picture

The original Ngram analysis (Part I) showed a compressed cultural inversion between 1938 and 1965, where "racism" went from a niche neologism to the dominant moral vocabulary of the English-speaking world. This extended analysis adds three dimensions:

Depth: The concept of "race" as a biological category is not ancient. It was invented in stages between 1449 (limpieza de sangre) and 1775 (Blumenbach's five races), reached its peak between 1850 and 1930, and was dismantled in a single generation. For most of human history, societies organized difference by culture, language, religion, and political membership, not by biology.

Breadth: The word "racism" originated in French (1894), spread to English (1902), and exploded simultaneously across five European languages in 1933. The Nazis did not invent racial ideology, but they forced every Western language to find a name for it. The 1933 spike is synchronous across all five corpora.

Agency: The moral framework for opposing racism was constructed with disproportionate contribution from Jewish intellectuals and organizations who had direct experience of racial ideology's consequences. Boas dismantled the science (1911-1942). Hirschfeld named the phenomenon (1933-34). Montagu drafted the UNESCO statement (1950). Lemkin gave international law the word "genocide" (1944). Adorno and the AJC-funded Frankfurt School psychologized prejudice (1950). Arendt traced its political genealogy (1951). Heschel marched with King (1965). This is not conspiracy; it is consequence. The people who suffered most from racial ideology built the intellectual and institutional tools to oppose it.

Bottom Line

Three thousand years of human difference. Five hundred years of "race." Thirty years of inversion.

Racial hierarchy was not an ancient universal. It was an invention of the early modern period (1449-1775), brought to its peak by 19th-century science (1850-1930), carried to its logical conclusion by the Nazis (1933-1945), and morally inverted in a compressed burst between 1945 and 1965. The word "racism" is younger than the telephone. The moral consensus that racism is wrong is younger than television. Both facts should give us perspective on how recently and how rapidly this change occurred, and how fragile it may still be.
