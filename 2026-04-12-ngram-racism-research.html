When Did Racism Become a Vice?

A Google Ngram frequency analysis tracking the moment English-language discourse reclassified racial hierarchy from common sense to moral failing. Four vocabularies, 220 years of data, one cultural inversion.

Verdict

The data strongly supports the hypothesis.

The word "racism" was functionally absent from English books before 1930, appeared measurably around 1935, and exploded after 1940. The old racial-science vocabulary ("racial stock," "racial characteristics," "inferior race") peaked between 1870 and 1930, then declined. The crossover, where condemnation language overtook hierarchy language in frequency, occurred between 1960 and 1965. This was not a gradual drift. It was a compressed cultural inversion spanning roughly 1938 to 1965, catalyzed by the Nazi regime, the Holocaust, and the American civil rights movement.

Methodology

All data was pulled from the Google Books Ngram Viewer JSON API using the English 2019 corpus (the most complete available) with smoothing factor 3. Smoothing reduces year-to-year noise while preserving real inflection points. Frequencies represent the fraction of all unigrams or bigrams in the corpus for a given year.

Twenty terms were tracked across four categories: condemnation vocabulary (when "racism is bad" got its words), racial-science vocabulary (the old default), moral counter-vocabulary (equality language), and a historical event overlay. Raw frequency values were extracted at key years using the timeseries array indices directly, avoiding any interpolation errors.

A critical note on interpretation: Ngram frequency tracks how often a term appears in published books. It does not directly measure public opinion, but it does track what authors, editors, and publishers considered worth printing. Shifts in Ngram frequency reflect shifts in the discourse of the literate class.

Key Finding "Racism" goes from 0.003 ppm in 1920 to 0.359 ppm in 1945: a 120x increase in 25 years. By 1970 it reaches 3.1 ppm, and by 2019 it stands at 10.2 ppm. The word was invented as a weapon and deployed with extraordinary speed. "Racial prejudice" and "racial discrimination" are the older, softer precursors, both appearing decades earlier, but "racism" overtakes them both by the late 1960s. The identity labels ("anti-racism," "antiracist") are a much later phenomenon, only reaching measurable frequency in the 1980s-90s and accelerating sharply after 2010 (the Ibram X. Kendi era).

Inflection analysis

  • Birth date of "racism": Measurably non-zero by 1930 (~0.015 ppm). The word existed in a few European languages earlier (French "racisme" from the 1890s), but enters English-language books in earnest around 1933-1935.
  • Sharpest acceleration: 1938-1945. The frequency quadruples between 1940 (0.157 ppm) and 1945 (0.359 ppm). This is WWII.
  • Second explosion: 1963-1970. From 1.0 ppm (1965) to 3.1 ppm (1970). This is the civil rights movement.
  • Status as of 2019: Still growing. No plateau in sight.

Key Finding The old racial-science vocabulary follows two distinct patterns. The explicit hierarchy terms ("inferior race," "superior race," "races of man") peak in the mid-to-late 19th century and decline through the 20th century. They were already fading before WWII. The "scientific" descriptive terms ("racial characteristics," "racial traits," "racial stock") peak later, around 1920-1940, during the height of the eugenics movement, and decline after. "Racial hygiene" is a special case: it peaks in 1940 (the Nazi period), collapses after 1945, then rises again from the 1990s onward as a subject of historical study, not advocacy. The post-2000 uptick in several of these terms likely reflects academic study of racism's history, not a return to racial science.

Decline patterns

  • "Races of man": Already in decline by 1880. A 19th-century anthropological phrase that was being replaced by more technical vocabulary even before moral objections arose. Peak: 0.228 ppm (1870), down to 0.036 ppm by 2019.
  • "Inferior race" / "superior race": Both peak in the 1850-1870 range (0.17-0.22 ppm) and decline through the 20th century. Interesting spike around 1940-1945 (the war focused attention on racial hierarchy claims), followed by steep post-war decline.
  • "Racial stock": The eugenics-era term. Near-zero before 1890, peaks at 0.092 ppm in 1940, halves by 1960, and stabilizes around 0.022 ppm. Its rise-and-fall tracks the eugenics movement almost exactly.
  • "Racial characteristics": Peak 1920-1930 at 0.164-0.170 ppm. The most "neutral" of the bunch, and accordingly the slowest to decline. Still at 0.064 ppm in 2019.

It was the moral sentiment; "civil rights" and "human rights" became the institutional program.

The Crossover

The central question: when did condemnation vocabulary overtake racial-science vocabulary in frequency?

Historical Overlay

Mapping the linguistic data against the events that shaped it.

1859 - Darwin's Origin of Species
No immediate Ngram effect. "Races of man" was already peaking (0.228 ppm by 1870). Darwin provided a scientific framework for existing racial thinking, but the vocabulary was already in place.
1900-1920 - Peak of the Eugenics Movement
"Racial characteristics" (0.092 to 0.164 ppm), "racial traits" (0.019 to 0.043 ppm), and "racial stock" (0.006 to 0.051 ppm) all surge. This is the high-water mark of "neutral" racial-science vocabulary. The word "racism" does not yet exist in measurable frequency.
1933-1945 - Nazi Regime / Holocaust
The single most important period in the data. "Racism" goes from 0.042 ppm (1935) to 0.359 ppm (1945), an 8.5x increase. "Racist" follows the same curve. "Racial hygiene" peaks (0.010 ppm, 1940) then collapses. The Nazis made the implicit explicit, and the world recoiled. This is the birth of racism-as-vice.
1948 - UN Universal Declaration of Human Rights
"Human rights" jumps from 0.796 ppm (1940) to 2.800 ppm (1945) to 3.945 ppm (1950). A 5x increase in a decade. The concept was institutionalized, given a document, given an organization. The moral framework for opposing racial hierarchy now had international legal backing.
1954 - Brown v. Board of Education
"Desegregation" explodes: 0.098 ppm (1950) to 0.830 ppm (1955) to 1.865 ppm (1960). "Civil rights" begins its ascent from legal term to movement label.
1964 - US Civil Rights Act
The peak moment of the second wave. "Racism" hits 1.0 ppm (1965) and triples to 3.1 ppm by 1970. "Civil rights" peaks at 7.8 ppm. "Racial discrimination" peaks at 2.1 ppm. The moral vocabulary is now dominant; the scientific vocabulary is in retreat.
1960s - African Decolonization
Amplifies the global reach of anti-racism discourse. "Racism" and "racist" grow not just in American English but across the English-language corpus. The concept extends from a domestic American concern to a global moral framework.
1980s-90s - Political Correctness Movement
"Racism" continues steady growth (3.5 to 5.4 ppm). "Anti-racism" becomes measurable for the first time (0.064 ppm by 1990). "Antiracist" emerges as an identity label (0.074 ppm by 1990). The inversion is now so complete that the discourse shifts from establishing that racism is bad to debating what counts as racism.
2010s - BLM / Renewed Antiracism
"Racism" reaches 10.2 ppm by 2019. "Antiracist" grows 4x from 2000 to 2019 (0.229 to 0.296 ppm). "Racial justice" triples (0.093 to 0.261 ppm). The discourse intensifies, but the fundamental inversion happened 70 years earlier. What the 2010s represent is a second-order effect: not "racism is bad" (already settled) but "racism is everywhere" (a new claim about scope).

Verdict

The hypothesis holds. The data shows three distinct phases:

Phase 1: The Old Default (1800-1930)

Racial hierarchy was discussed as a matter of natural history, not morality. The vocabulary was descriptive ("racial characteristics," "races of man," "inferior race"). The word "racism" did not exist in English. "Racial prejudice" and "racial discrimination" were beginning to appear (mainly in legal and reform contexts), but they were niche terms, not moral thunderbolts. The dominant assumption was that races differ in capacity, and this was about as controversial as noting that dog breeds differ in temperament.

Phase 2: The Inversion (1933-1965)

The Nazis forced the question. By taking racial hierarchy to its logical conclusion, the Third Reich made the implicit position explicit and repulsive. The word "racism" entered English as a label for what the Nazis were doing. Within 15 years, the moral framework had flipped: the 1948 UN Declaration established human equality as an international norm, and "racism" became the name for violating that norm. The American civil rights movement (1954-1968) completed the transformation domestically. By 1965, "racism" was 10x more frequent than "inferior race." The old default had become the new sin.

Phase 3: The New Default (1965-present)

Once the inversion was complete, the discourse shifted. The question was no longer whether racism was bad (that was settled) but what counted as racism, how deep it ran, and what should be done about it. "Racism" continued to grow in frequency throughout this period, from 3.1 ppm (1970) to 10.2 ppm (2019). New vocabulary emerged: "anti-racism" (1980s), "antiracist" (1990s), "racial justice" (2010s). The 2010s BLM movement did not create the moral framework; it intensified and expanded it.

Bottom Line

The cultural inversion occurred in the 1940s.

The word "racism" entered English around 1933-1935, became common by 1945, and became dominant by 1965. The old racial-science vocabulary peaked between 1870 and 1930 and was in decline before WWII even began, but the decline accelerated sharply after 1945. The crossover, where condemnation language exceeded hierarchy language, happened between 1945 and 1950. This was a compressed event, not a gradual drift. The entire inversion took roughly 30 years: from near-zero "racism" in 1935 to a 22:1 ratio over the old vocabulary by 1970. Nothing in the Ngram data suggests that moral opposition to racial hierarchy was a significant presence in English-language discourse before the 20th century. It was, as the hypothesis proposes, a 20th-century invention.

Limitations

  • Books corpus bias: Google Ngrams covers published books, not newspapers, speeches, pamphlets, or oral culture. The moral shift may have occurred earlier in spoken discourse or political rhetoric than in books.
  • English-only: This analysis covers only the English 2019 corpus. The word "racisme" appeared in French before "racism" appeared in English. A cross-language analysis might identify earlier inflection points.
  • Frequency vs. sentiment: Ngrams track how often a word appears, not how it is used. A rising frequency for "racism" in the 1940s could reflect both condemnation of racism and discussion of racism as a concept (including neutral or even sympathetic usage). However, the simultaneous decline of "inferior race" and "racial stock" supports the interpretation that the discourse was shifting against racial hierarchy, not just talking about it more.
  • Post-2000 academic effect: Some terms ("racial hygiene," "inferior race") show small upticks after 2000, likely driven by historical and academic study of racism rather than renewed advocacy. The Ngram data cannot distinguish between these uses.
  • Smoothing artifacts: Smoothing=3 can blur very sharp transitions. The true inflection points may be 1-2 years narrower than they appear in the smoothed data.

Further Research

  • Cross-language comparison: Run the same analysis on the French, German, and Spanish corpora. Was the inversion synchronous across languages or did it originate in one and spread?
  • Newspaper corpus: If a historical newspaper Ngram-equivalent exists, test whether the shift appeared earlier in journalism than in books.
  • Sentiment analysis: Use a fine-grained corpus (COHA or COCA) to distinguish between condemnatory uses of "racism" and neutral/descriptive uses over time.
  • Counter-terms: Track terms like "race realism," "racial differences," and "human biodiversity" to map the post-inversion counter-movement in the 1990s-2010s.
  • Legal vs. moral framing: Track when "racism" shifts from a political/legal concept to a moral/character concept, possibly by tracking collocations ("racist policies" vs. "racist person").
  • The role of specific authors: Ruth Benedict's "Race: Science and Politics" (1940) and Gunnar Myrdal's "An American Dilemma" (1944) are often cited as pivotal. Can their influence be detected in the Ngram data?
