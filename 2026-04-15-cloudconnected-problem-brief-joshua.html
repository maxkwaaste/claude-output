01The setup

Dutch MSP, ~15 years in market, clients in the Netherlands plus offices in Hong Kong, Japan, the US, and France. Mostly remote workforce with a strong freelance engineering bench. Anastasiya joined to install processes from scratch — her work, together with the CTO, lifted billable profitability ~67% in six months while headcount dropped. The processes are good. The tooling underneath them is not.

02What's actually broken

  • 01
    Engineers are double-booked between help desk and projects, every day. Most engineers split time across both. Project commitments lose to whatever ticket is loudest. Anastasiya's words: violations happen "on a daily basis."
  • 02
    Zero visibility for the PM until it's too late. Anastasiya only learns mid-day that a senior engineer is buried in help-desk work and won't hit the project deadline. Her days then collapse into firefighting — chasing people instead of running the project.
  • 03
    The CEO and management cannot see the trade-off. Because help desk lives in Autotask and projects live in Toggl Plan, no one at the top can see how project hours are being eaten by ticket work. There is no enforceable balance.
  • 04
    Capacity planning happens in side conversations. Looking at the next month — finances, bookable hours, who is free for what — is currently a manual back-and-forth between project managers. There is no shared capacity surface.
  • 05
    Senior engineers are stressed by the chaos. The dual-commitment problem lands on the small group of seniors who cover both sides. Anastasiya flagged this as a retention concern, not just an efficiency one.
  • 06
    Templated project work isn't being reused as a system. Most of their work — network and infra updates, migrations, SharePoint rollouts, new service introductions — is repeatable. They have SOPs, but the planning tool doesn't templatize them, so every project gets re-built by hand.
  • 07
    Billing depends on a fragile external automation. Invoices currently fire automatically from a custom pipeline pulling from Toggl. If we replace Toggl, billing has to come along — they don't want to keep two integrations alive for one financial flow.
  • 08
    Freelancer licensing is awkward. Some freelance engineers do project work one month a quarter, never touch the help desk, and don't justify a full Autotask license. They need a path that lets these people work in Proxuma without paying for Autotask seats they won't use.

03What she said, in her own words

"I have zero visibility. I only know mid of the day, when a senior engineer tells me they spent the entire day on tickets. It's very chaotic and it creates a lot of stress for the senior engineers who work both on the projects and on the help desk." — Anastasiya, Project Manager, CloudConnected
"Our current setup creates a lot of frustrations for the management. We're looking for something that can be integrated with Autotask and help us improve — first of all, with the engineering commitments, the deadlines, and everything else, because right now it's quite heavy with planning between several tools." — Anastasiya, opening the call

04What they're hoping Proxuma does

  • →
    One surface for plan + execute, sitting on top of Autotask. They want to keep the desk where it is. They want one place to plan engineers, see the help-desk vs project trade-off, and roll out templated projects.
  • →
    A capacity view the CEO can read. Management needs to see in advance whether the next month is overcommitted, by engineer, before deadlines slip.
  • →
    Templated project rollouts. Migrations, infra updates, SharePoint — drop a template, get a planned project, ready to assign.
  • →
    Billing flow that doesn't break when Toggl leaves. Either Proxuma carries the billing, or there's a clean export the existing invoice pipeline can keep consuming.
  • →
    A light seat option for occasional freelancers. A way to let project-only freelancers in without the cost of a full Autotask license.
Bottom line

This is a process-mature MSP that has outgrown its tooling. They have already proven that discipline pays — 67% billable profit lift in six months. They are not buying tooling to find their process. They are buying tooling to stop their process from being eroded by tool fragmentation. Proxuma's core promise — one plane over Autotask for planning, capacity, and project execution — maps almost one-to-one onto what Anastasiya described.
