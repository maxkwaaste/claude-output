PCOS & TTC at 29: Dr. Natalie Crawford's Playbook

Patient profile: 29 years old, PCOS diagnosis, off hormonal birth control 3+ years, 7 months trying to conceive without success. Below is what Dr. Natalie Crawford (double board-certified OBGYN + REI, Fora Fertility, host of As a Woman) would likely recommend based on her public positions.

Bottom line

Don't wait another five months

Crawford's guidance is explicit: if you have irregular periods or a known condition like PCOS, the standard "try for a year first" rule doesn't apply — see an OBGYN or fertility doctor now [1]. Three years off birth control rules out post-pill delay. Seven months with PCOS is a signal worth acting on, not a failure. At 29 with this picture, the odds of a good outcome are high with the right workup and treatment.

1. See a fertility specialist (REI) now

The textbook rule is 12 months of trying under 35 before seeing a specialist. Crawford carves out an exception for women with irregular cycles, known PCOS, endometriosis, or suspected tubal issues — she recommends going straight to a reproductive endocrinologist rather than waiting out the year [1]. PCOS + 7 months clearly qualifies.

2. Confirm you're actually ovulating

PCOS patients often don't ovulate every cycle even when periods look regular. Crawford's tracking protocol:

  • LH strips (OPKs) between 10am and 2pm, not first thing in the morning. LH surges overnight and needs hours to show in urine [2].
  • On the first positive OPK, have sex that day and the next [2].
  • Pair OPKs with a second method — BBT charting or a progesterone blood draw ~7 days after suspected ovulation — to confirm ovulation actually happened [2].
  • Apps alone are not reliable with PCOS cycles [2].

3. Full workup to request

Blood work

Day-3 FSH, LH, estradiol, AMH. TSH, prolactin. Free and total testosterone, DHEA-S, 17-OHP (to rule out non-classical CAH). Fasting insulin + glucose, HbA1c. Vitamin D. These are the panels Crawford names as standard in her PCOS Q&A content [3].

Imaging

Transvaginal ultrasound for antral follicle count and to confirm polycystic morphology. HSG (hysterosalpingogram) to check tubal patency before starting ovulation induction.

Partner semen analysis — non-negotiable

Crawford repeatedly flags that male-factor workup gets skipped in favor of putting the woman through everything first. Roughly 30–40% of infertility is male-factor. A semen analysis is cheap, fast, and should happen in parallel with the female workup [4].

4. First-line treatment: letrozole

For PCOS-related anovulation, Crawford follows the current standard: letrozole, not clomiphene. This is backed by the PPCOS II trial in NEJM, which showed letrozole produced more live births than clomid in PCOS patients [5], and the 2023 International PCOS Guidelines now recommend letrozole as first-line pharmacologic therapy for anovulatory infertility [6]. Typical protocol: 2.5mg days 3–7, with monitoring ultrasound, paired with timed intercourse or IUI.

5. Lifestyle and supplements

  • Myo-inositol + D-chiro-inositol, 40:1 ratio (2g myo twice daily). Improves insulin sensitivity and restores ovulation in a meaningful share of PCOS patients — Crawford regularly discusses it as a reasonable first add-on [6], and the evidence base is decent [7].
  • Vitamin D if deficient — target ~40–60 ng/mL. Low D is very common in PCOS and linked to worse insulin sensitivity.
  • Prenatal with methylfolate, started now.
  • Metformin if insulin resistance is present on labs — often combined with letrozole in the PCOS setting.
  • Resistance training + protein-forward diet over aggressive cardio and calorie restriction. Crawford emphasizes building muscle and insulin sensitivity, not weight loss as a target [8].
  • Sleep and stress are not optional. Both directly move cortisol, insulin, and LH [8].
Reassurance 29 is a strong age. PCOS is the most common cause of ovulatory infertility and also one of the most treatable. Crawford's framing: this is a diagnosis with a playbook, not a dead end [8].
