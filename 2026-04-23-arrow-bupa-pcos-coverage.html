Bottom line

Bupa won't fund the Crawford playbook. Run NHS + self-pay in parallel and use Bupa for the symptom side door.

The Bupa Select policy Arrow Global offers excludes fertility treatment, IVF, IUI, fertility investigations, donor anything, and ongoing chronic-condition management. It does not exclude acute gynaecological symptoms presenting for the first time. The play: use the NHS pathway for the fertility workup proper (it's actually well-set-up for PCOS), self-pay the items NHS won't move fast enough on (letrozole monitoring, semen analysis turnaround, supplements), and use the Bupa policy as the fast lane for any acute symptom that isn't framed as "we are trying to conceive".

1. What the Arrow Bupa policy says, in plain English

The Bupa Select policy guide for Arrow Global (BINS reference 11438, version starting after 1 May 2024) is a standard mid-market UK group PMI. Coverage is gated by what the membership certificate ticks AND what the policy guide explicitly excludes. Three exclusions are decisive here:

Exclusion 5 — Birth control, conception and sexual problems
"Treatment isn't covered for: contraception, sterilisation or termination of pregnancy; sexual problems; or fertility treatment such as assisted reproduction, fertility investigations, IVF, surrogacy, harvesting of donor eggs or donor sperm." This is the killing blow. Anything the consultant codes as a fertility workup gets denied.
Exclusion 6 — Chronic conditions
PCOS is, by definition, chronic. Bupa won't pay for ongoing management. They will pay for diagnosis of an "acute flare-up" of an underlying condition, which is the wedge.
Exclusion 23 — Pregnancy and childbirth
Pregnancy itself, antenatal care, and delivery aren't covered. A short list of pregnancy emergencies (ectopic pregnancy, post-partum haemorrhage, miscarriage requiring surgical intervention) is.
Exclusion 1 — Hormone-related, with one carve-out
Anything framed as natural hormonal change isn't covered. Carve-out: an acute condition arising from hormonal change, like menorrhagia (heavy bleeding), is covered. This is relevant because PCOS often presents as menorrhagia or oligomenorrhoea.

Worth checking with HR directly Three things the policy pack doesn't tell you, all of which materially change the answer:
  1. Does Arrow co-pay or fully pay the premium? If it's salary-sacrifice with full employee cost, the value at your wife's age is questionable for fertility purposes specifically (almost zero) but reasonable for general health (digital GP + fast specialist access).
  2. Is there a separate Carrot, Fertifa, Maven, or similar fertility benefit? Major UK employers increasingly offer these on top of PMI as a standalone wallet (typically £5-25k per employee for fertility). Arrow is PE-owned and ~1,000+ employees, well within the band that buys this. If they have it, that's the real prize.
  3. Is there a higher Bupa tier with fertility add-ons? Rare in UK PMI but exists. Some Bupa group plans add a one-off fertility allowance (typically £3-15k) as an upsell tier. Worth one email.

4. The NHS pathway is the real anchor

UK NHS funding for IVF varies by Integrated Care Board (your postcode). The general picture for someone at 29 with PCOS:

  • NICE recommends up to 3 full IVF cycles for women under 40 who haven't conceived after 2 years of trying. PCOS-related anovulation that hasn't responded to ovulation induction is an explicitly eligible route.
  • Common ICB criteria: woman's BMI 19-30, neither partner has children from a previous relationship, non-smokers, age cutoff varies (usually 39 or 42).
  • NHS will fund the workup itself (AMH, semen analysis, HSG, ultrasounds) regardless of whether IVF is later approved. This is the workup Bupa won't pay for.
  • Letrozole for ovulation induction is routinely prescribed on the NHS for PCOS, off-label, in line with the 2023 international PCOS guidelines.

Time-to-NHS-clinic varies wildly (weeks to ~6 months depending on ICB). Get on the list now even if you're considering self-pay in parallel.

2

3
NHS GP
Book NHS GP for fertility referral. Be explicit about PCOS + 7 months TTC. They can fast-track.
4
Bupa side door
Use Blua Health digital GP for an open referral to private gynae for "irregular cycles + suspected PCOS". Diagnostic, not fertility.
5
Self-pay gaps
Semen analysis (~£100-200 private, days not weeks), inositol, prenatal, vitamin D. Total ~£50/month ongoing.
6
Track
Mid-day OPKs + day-21 progesterone bloods (NHS GP can order). Confirms whether ovulation is the bottleneck.
7
Letrozole
Once workup is done, NHS or private gynae prescribes letrozole 2.5mg days 3-7. Cheap drug, key intervention.
8
IVF only if needed
Letrozole resolves PCOS anovulation in a meaningful share of patients. Don't pre-commit to IVF spend.
