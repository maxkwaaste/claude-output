What "As Good As Teamwork.com" Actually Means

A teardown of teamwork.com's marketing site, mapped against Proxuma's current frontend stack and brand, with a recommendation for where leverage lives.

Framing Teamwork.com is not visually loud. It feels expensive because of seven restrained, high-discipline choices — typography, one accent color, section rhythm, self-hosted motion, animated numbers, megamenus that convert, and microcopy written by a human. Each of those is portable to Proxuma without copying the site.

What I found in your codebase

Proxuma's frontend is Nuxt 3 + Vue with Tailwind, Pinia, and an in-house UI kit (pages/ui-kit). It has the machinery to build something this polished — no need to start a new stack. The brand tokens in tailwind.config.js point at a dated palette for a marketing site though: lots of status-state colors (new, complete, waiting, progress, escalate, approved, denied) that make sense for the app UI but aren't a marketing story.

What teamwork.com actually does

1. A distinct display typeface
Headings are Americane Condensed — a narrow, editorial sans. Body is Work Sans. Self-hosted, not Google Fonts. Every competitor uses Inter. Teamwork reads as "brand" before you read a word.
2. One saturated accent, used with restraint
Hot magenta #ff61c7 appears only on inline keyword emphasis in headlines and CTA accents. Everything else is navy and off-white. Discipline, not variety, is why it reads polished.
3. Alternating dark/light section bands
Every major section flips tone. Dark sections act as display cases for glowing product UI. Gives the page implicit chapter structure.
4. Animated numbers via <number-flow>
Count-up stats and price toggles animate. ~2KB web component. Reads as "this product moves" instead of "this is a brochure."
5. Self-hosted silent mp4 loops, not screenshots or YouTube
Each feature gets a small silent clip on Storyblok CDN. No YouTube chrome, loads fast, makes every feature feel alive.
6. Megamenus that double as landing pages
Every dropdown has a capabilities column, a featured resource card (masterclass / webinar), twin Book demo / Start trial CTAs pinned inside, and competitor comparison links (vs Monday, vs Asana, vs ClickUp).
7. Microcopy written by a human
"30 day trial. No credit card required" not "Free trial." "Save 29%" not "Save up to 30%." Stat captions ("fewer mystery gaps") have personality. Plan descriptions name a persona, not a feature count.

Bonus move: Ask-an-LLM buttons

At the top of the product page: Ask ChatGPT / Ask Claude / Ask Perplexity / Ask Grok / Ask Google AI. They pre-bake the handoff because they know buyers paste landing pages into LLMs. For MSPs — who are exactly that persona — this would land hard on proxuma.io.

What this means for Proxuma specifically

You don't need to clone teamwork.com. You need a Dutch-MSP version of the same discipline, not the same visual. Here's how that translates.

Typography

Retire Inter on the marketing site. Pair a distinct display face with a neutral body. Candidates worth exploring: Söhne, Neue Haas Grotesk Display, or a condensed serif for editorial feel (e.g., Söhne Schmal, Canela Condensed). Body stays utilitarian (Inter is fine there, or IBM Plex Sans). Montserrat from the current system should probably retire — it's everywhere now and reads generic.

Color

Pick one saturated accent. Teal #0f766e is already alive in the Power BI reports and aligns with your stated preference. Use it only for inline headline emphasis and CTAs. Everything else: Navy #1B365D + off-white #FAFAF9. Retire #00B7FF and #49C481 from the marketing surface (keep them in-app).

Motion

Record silent mp4 loops of your actual Power BI reports — the AI forecaster panel updating, DAX chips animating in, the report overview scrolling. You already have the world's nicest Power BI reports (page 3956 golden standard). That is the hero imagery, not stock product shots.

Megamenu

Four dropdowns: Product, Solutions (MSP / IT Services / Support Desk), Why Proxuma (vs N-able, vs ConnectWise BrightGauge, vs Auvik — these are real MSP buyer comparisons), Resources (KB articles, report gallery, masterclass). Every dropdown ends with Book demo / Start trial.

Microcopy

Humanizer rules already apply to your output. Extend them to the site: no "powerful", no "seamless", no "up to". Every reassurance line, stat caption, and plan description gets rewritten as a concrete sentence.

The Ask-an-LLM move

This is cheap to ship, distinctive, and MSPs will absolutely paste Proxuma into Claude to decide whether to trial. Put the buttons at the top of the product page and the homepage.

Bottom line

The leverage is in discipline, not features

Teamwork.com has 16,000+ customers and a brand team. You don't need either to land 80% of the perceived quality. Buy one display typeface, commit to one accent color, alternate section tones, film your real Power BI reports, and rewrite the microcopy by hand. That's most of the gap closed — with your existing Nuxt stack, no new infrastructure, and a budget measured in hours of craft, not contractor invoices.

The remaining 20% — the custom web components (number-flow, tabbed tours), the megamenu-as-landing-page, the competitor comparison pages — is buildable in your current Nuxt 3 setup without new dependencies. The Claude design tool gives you a visual scratchpad to iterate mockups fast; the production code goes into proxuma-frontend directly.

Open questions before we scope this

  1. Is this a proxuma.io marketing-site rebuild, or does it also cover the in-app dashboard? (They're different scopes.)
  2. Is proxuma.io currently served from WordPress/SiteGround, or is there already a Nuxt marketing site I haven't seen? (The proxuma-frontend repo looks like the app, not the marketing site.)
  3. Budget for a paid display typeface license (~€400–2,000), or stick to free-but-distinctive (e.g., Fraunces, DM Serif)?
  4. Dutch/English bilingual from day one, or English first then NL?
  5. Are the Ask-an-LLM buttons in or out? (I think yes, but flagging.)

Next step: pick one of those to answer, and I'll start the clarifying questions one at a time before we design anything.
